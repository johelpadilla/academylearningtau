# Relationship with Critical Transitions Theory and Critical Slowing Down

## The Classical Framework

The theory of **critical transitions** (Scheffer et al.) states that systems with alternative states or fold bifurcations can show generic signals of proximity to the threshold. The most cited mechanism is **critical slowing down (CSD)**: the dominant eigenvalue of the Jacobian approaches zero and recovery slows down.

This motivates univariate EWS (variance, AR1, etc.). The framework is powerful when:

- the potential model is a good metaphor,
- there is a slow control parameter,
- the noise is moderate and additive,
- the observation is essentially univariate or projectable to a dominant mode.

## Where the Tau/RECD Paradigm Aligns

1. **Same scientific question:** Can we anticipate or characterize a regime shift?
2. **Same demand for falsifiability:** nulls, surrogates, controls.
3. **Interest in memory and correlation:** CSD predicts more memory; ordinal measures also see changes in temporal structure, but through **ranks and conjunctions**.

## Where it Separates (Productively)

| Aspect | Classical CSD | Tau + RECD |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| Assumed Mechanism | Slowing down of a mode | Multivariate relational reorganization |
| Canonical Signature | var↑, AR1↑ | Δτ_s and Δexcess3 (contextual sign) |
| Time | external clock | internal clock (RECD) |
| Unit | often 1 series | N≥2 + Φ levels |

In pre-VF cardiac data, "textbook" CSD **does not** describe AR1 well (which usually drops). Even so, there is an unequivocal clinical transition. The relational framework **still sees a signal**. This does not "refute" CSD; it shows that **it is not the only generic pathway** in physiological systems.
